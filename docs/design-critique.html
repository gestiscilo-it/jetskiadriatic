Strong but not yet exceptional. The Riviera language is genuinely executed — wave dividers, italic-serif emphasis, numbered card overlays, persistent mobile chrome — and that puts the page well above the generic-Mediterranean median. What pulls the score down is infrastructure hygiene: 11+ commented-out section blocks, two parallel design-token systems (--navy and --abisso) declaring the same hex values, and ad-hoc inline styles where utility classes would belong. Fix those and the page lands at 8+ across the board.

Philosophy consistency

7 · Strong Coherent direction with named drift

Does the artifact pick a clear direction and stick to it through every micro-decision?

The Riviera language executes consistently at the visible layer — wave-divider primitive at every section transition (index.html lines 138-145, 283-288, 320-325, 467-474, 537-542, 591-597, 670-677, always two paths: fill + cyan stroke), italic-serif <em> emphasis in every headline (lines 150, 226, 295, 478-481, 506), numbered .exp-n overlays on every card grid. That's a unified thesis you can read in five minutes. The drift sits underneath: app.css declares two parallel :root blocks — lines 6-46 (--navy, --cyan, --ink) and lines 2096-2130 (--abisso, --tramonto, --acqua, --spuma) — declaring overlapping hex values under different names. One philosophy, two languages. The .timbro and .wave-splash elements use 5+ inline-style declarations (lines 330-344) where utility classes belong. And 11+ commented-out section-head blocks (lines 172-178, 363-393 entire gallery, 397-403, 503) are committed mid-redesign — dead code that signals unsettled decisions.

KEEP
The wave-divider commitment — every light/dark transition gets two paths, fill + tramonto stroke. Don't soften it to a CSS gradient.
FIX
Consolidate the two :root blocks into one. Pick the Italian naming (--abisso, --tramonto) — it carries brand voice — and delete the English duplicates.
QUICK WIN
Delete the 11+ commented-out section blocks. If they're being kept "in case", move them to a docs/parking-lot.html.

Visual hierarchy

6 · Functional Section interiors clean, page top stacks

Can a stranger figure out what to read first, second, third — without being told?

Inside individual sections, hierarchy is excellent: hero (lines 147-167) reads h1 → lead → primary CTA + ghost CTA in a clear cascade; experience cards (lines 182-216) follow the canonical number → image → title → body → tags → price+CTA pattern with no ambiguity; FAQ accordion uses numbered prefixes plus weight 700 questions to clearly distinguish question from body. The page-level problem is the chrome stack on first paint: info-strip (line 23, navy thin bar) → topbar with tabs (line 47) → cat-bar (line 76, sticky bottom on mobile / under topbar on desktop) → then products feed (line 87) → then hero (line 128). Four chrome layers before the hero on mobile, totaling ~140px of fixed-height navigation before content begins. The manifesto section (lines 346-358) has three competing visual peaks — the headline "Noleggi. Tramonti. Escursioni. Yacht.", four large stat numbers (9 / 6 / 4 / 2), and a serif "C'è un solo posto" line — none clearly subordinate to the others.

KEEP
Card-internal hierarchy and FAQ numbering. The .exp grid is a textbook clean tier.
FIX
The page-top chrome stack. Either make info-strip dismissible after first view, or merge cat-bar visually into topbar on desktop so there are 2 layers, not 4.
QUICK WIN
In the manifesto section, demote the four stat blocks to a single horizontal strip below the headline, weight 600 not 800. Let "C'è un solo posto" be the visual peak.

DIMENSION 03

Detail execution

7 · Strong Surface-clean, infrastructure-owed

The 90/10 stuff — alignment, leading, kerning at large sizes, image framing, edge-case spacing.

The visible details hold: every wave divider uses identical SVG geometry (viewBox="0 0 1440 80", preserveAspectRatio="none", stroke-width="2.5", stroke-linecap="round"); the numbered .exp-n overlay is consistent across all card grids; outline-only .exp-tags match across rental and love-experience grids; the kicker pattern (lowercase in markup, uppercase via CSS, with a 24×2px tramonto dash prefix) is uniform. Where execution slips is one level deeper: the main hero's wavy edges (lines 138-145) and the yacht hero's wavy edges (lines 467-474) are copy-pasted SVG — same path data, two locations, no shared partial. Same story for the ~15 trailing-arrow icons inside CTAs across the page — each is inline SVG with identical path data. The .timbro and .wave-splash decorations rely on inline style="top:50px;right:6vw;..." declarations (lines 330-344) instead of utility classes with custom properties — readable now, brittle for the next adopter. And app.css at 3 127 lines suggests organic accretion rather than a designed token cascade.

KEEP
The wave-divider geometry as the canonical pattern. Every viewBox, every stroke width, every linecap.
FIX
Define one <svg id="riviera-icons"><symbol id="arrow-r">...</symbol> at the page foot, replace the ~15 inline arrow SVGs with <svg><use href="#arrow-r"/></svg>. Saves ~2KB and removes a maintenance trap.
QUICK WIN
Convert .timbro and .wave-splash inline styles to utility classes with --top, --right, --w custom properties. Same flexibility, reusable.

Functionality

8 · Strong Defensively engineered for the rental loop

Does the artifact work for its intended use? Click targets, nav, edge cases, mobile fallback.

This is the highest-scoring dimension and earns it. The mobile shell is persistently navigable: topbar slides up past products via body.is-past-products .topbar { top: -200px } (CSS lines 191-194) but cat-bar stays fixed at the bottom — the user is never lost. The .status pill above the topbar shows live operational state (open / whatsapp-only / closed) with a pulsing dot animation, not decoration but signal. The 7-day forecast widget (lines 519-525) ships skeleton placeholders for async loading rather than CLS-inducing blank space. The empty state for filtered cards (lines 101-107) is wired. The WhatsApp FAB (lines 113-115) is always-reachable with safe-area awareness. Booking flow (extensive .bk-* classes around CSS line 800) has steppers, time-grid pills, extras list, consent checkbox — full multi-step form. Map (line 631) + Google-Maps directions CTA (line 635) + tappable phone link (line 36) close the contact loop. The few items that pull from 9 to 8: no skip-to-main-content link visible, the iframe-embedded map needs a fallback for users with iframe-blocking, and the display: optional Google Fonts strategy risks FOIT on slow connections.

KEEP
The persistent mobile cat-bar with topbar slide-up. This pattern is rare and works — don't replace with a unified header.
FIX
Add a skip-to-main-content link before the info strip — required for keyboard users navigating past four chrome layers.
QUICK WIN
Swap display: optional to display: swap on the Google Fonts URL, or self-host Archivo + Instrument Serif WOFF2 with a preload hint. Either eliminates FOIT.

DIMENSION 05

Innovation

7 · Strong Two memorable moves, conventional genre

Does this push past the median? Is there one element that makes people lean in?

Two genuinely distinctive moves carry this dimension. First, the commitment to the wave: most coastal/Mediterranean sites reach for a thin border-bottom or a linear-gradient divider. This site commits to a single SVG primitive — fill path plus cyan stroke — at every section transition, and uses it at the top and bottom of every video hero. That's a structural design decision masquerading as a flourish, and it pays off: the page reads as one continuous coastline. Second, the body[data-active-tab="experience"] selector (CSS line 778) swaps the entire primary-CTA gradient from navy → navy to rose → rose, switching the brand register without a second stylesheet — clever multi-brand handling. The italic-serif <em> emphasis pattern is recognizable and underused in the rental/booking genre. Where innovation is bounded: the broader direction (Italian coastal premium leisure with navy + cyan + warm accent) is well-trodden — Le Sirenuse, Rosewood, Eolian Islands sites all live in this neighborhood. The execution is original; the genre is not. There's no kinetic typography, no scroll-bound storytelling, no novel form interaction — and that's appropriate for a booking site, but it caps the score.

KEEP
The body[data-active-tab] brand-register toggle. This is the kind of move you'd steal — keep using it.
FIX
The italic-serif emphasis is great but predictable — every headline has it. Try one or two headlines without italic emphasis to make the move land harder when it appears.
QUICK WIN
Add one scroll-tied moment: the manifesto section's "C'è un solo posto" sentence could fade-in word-by-word as it enters viewport. Earned, not grafted on.
